        TELL:
            Supervised Learning:
                Question: How can a model learn a mapping from inputs to known 
                          correct outputs?
                Issue: The model needs labeled data, & performance depends 
                       heavily on label quality & coverage.
                Example: the input might be the age & mileage of a second-h& car, 
                         & the output might be the price in dollars.

            Self-Supervised Learning:
                Question: How can a model learn useful representations from 
                          unlabeled data by creating its own training signal?
                Issue: There are no human-provided labels, so the model must 
                       define proxy tasks that force it to learn meaningful 
                       structure.
                Example: Masking words in a sentence & training a model to 
                         predict the missing words.
            
            Un-Supervised Learning:
                Question: How can a model discover structure in data without labels?
                Issue: There is no explicit target output, so the model must infer 
                       patterns such as clusters, latent factors, or compressed 
                       representations.
                Example: Grouping customers into segments based only on purchase 
                         behavior.

            Reinforcement Learning:
                Question: How can an agent learn what to do by interacting with an 
                          environment & receiving rewards?
                Issue: Feedback is delayed, sparse, & depends on sequences of 
                       actions rather than single predictions.
                Example: Learning to play a game by trying actions & improving 
                         based on wins & losses.

        TELL:
            so to calculate the error, to tell the NN how wrong it was, we get our 
            labes for this example, & see how different the NN's answer is from
            this example's labels:

            the simplest way is to just subtract the NN's answer from the labels,
            but that way if one error is too negative & another error is too 
            positive, they cancel out to give no error! when they should've given 
            double their individual errors! being in two different ways doesn't 
            cancel out to being right! 

            so when we subtract the the answer & label, we square the result!
            this way all the errors become positive so to not cancel-out each other.

            Alright, we subtract the NN's predictions from the labels, then square the 
            differences, now we have 10 positive numbers each telling us how wrong
            the NN is in one way or another, we want a single number for our loss 
            landscape, so we take an average of these 10 losses, sum them up & 
            divide by how many there are. 
            the number we get is the NN's loss calculated for a single example.

            This way of calculating loss is called "The Mean Squared Error"

        why NNs:
            Although there are thousands of different methods for making computers 
            act intelligently & learn from data, this perticular set of methods
            that are based on NNs are by far the most successful approaches, and
            no matter how clever the other methods are, with enough data & compute,
            a NN of sufficent size will be able to implement that clever algorithm,
            even in the extreme, a NN can simulate the mind of the inventor that 
            came-up with that method or algorithm...

            lets go even further than that, imagine a point in the future that all
            software is written in NNs, your phone's operating system, apps & websites
            are all a neural network that takes thouch, voice, images & internet 
            packets & renders the pixels on your screen or vibrates the speaker!


            this vision of all software being in form f a NN is motivated by this:
            as consumers & businesses we want things bo be efficient, to consume 
            less & give more, so we write more & more efficient algorithms, 
            what-ever business has the most efficient systems will win. 


            a traditional programmer had to know the rules & inner-workings of that 
            function which turned inputs into outputs, so the efficiency of the 
            program was limited by how clever the human programmer was!

            but in the case for NNs, the only thing you need is examples of inputs &
            outputs, the NN itself during training & learning finds the most efficient
            algorithm or process & implements it in its parameters !

            this gives rise to the bitter but true saying that:
                Gradient-Descent is the world's best programmer.
